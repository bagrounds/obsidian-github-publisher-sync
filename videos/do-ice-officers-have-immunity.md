## 🤔 Evaluation  
  
- ⚖️ While the video discusses the potential for state prosecution, the Congressional Research Service report titled Qualified Immunity: An Overview by the Library of Congress notes that the legal bar for overcoming immunity is exceptionally high, requiring a violation of clearly established law.  
- 🔍 This suggests that while states have the power to try, winning is statistically rare.  
- 📚 Further exploration of the Supremacy Clause and the 1890 Neagle decision would provide a better understanding of why federal courts often block state charges against agents.  
  
## ❓ Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)  
  
### 👮 Q: Do ICE agents have total immunity from all state crimes?  
  
🛡️ A: No, federal officers do not have absolute immunity and must comply with federal laws and act reasonably \[[04:41](http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KCFbXjqq0U4&t=281)].  
  
### ⚖️ Q: Can a state like Minnesota actually arrest a federal officer?  
  
📝 A: Yes, states or counties can file criminal charges, though the officer will likely attempt to move the case to federal court \[[05:15](http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KCFbXjqq0U4&t=315)].  
  
### 🏛️ Q: What is the historical precedent for states checking federal power?  
  
📜 A: States have historically used criminal charges to push back against federal actions during Prohibition and the Fugitive Slave Act era \[[09:05](http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KCFbXjqq0U4&t=545)].  
  
### 🚔 Q: Why is ICE operating in suburban neighborhoods instead of just borders?  
  
🔄 A: The current administration has shifted resources toward a mass deportation campaign targeting interior cities \[[28:33](http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KCFbXjqq0U4&t=1713)].
